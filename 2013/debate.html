SUMMARY

 

Much more detail is below, but because it is lengthy I provide a summary here.  I reviewed three of the seven referenced surveys to try and understand objectively my confidence in their results.  I found that each survey points to an assumed truth, and then states that Fox News viewers are most likely to disagree with this assumed truth.  Indeed, if the assumed truth is true, these Fox News viewers are misinformed, which is the assertion that was made.  However, the accuracy of this assertion in each survey depends on the assumed truth being true.  What if it is not true? 

 

In Link 2, it is straightforward to show that the assumed truth that no ”death panels” exist in the ObamaCare legislation is at worst incorrect, and is at best highly ambiguous.  Therefore someone who is questioning the truth of the Administration’s assertions about the law in this regard is not misinformed.  She is properly questioning, to try and find the truth.

 

In Link 7, I showed that the global warming facts on which the survey hinges are in doubt and therefore are being subject to scientific review, but that the politics of the situation are hindering progress on the reviews.  Again, since the survey hinges on this assumed truth, the outcome of the survey as to who is actually ”misinformed” is in question until there is again scientific agreement on the degree of human contribution to global warming.

 

In Link 5, the study is shown to depend on a circular argument.  It’s crafted carefully to hide the circle, but it’s circular nonetheless.  No one can draw valid logical conclusions from a circular argument.

 

So, what is Fair and Balanced Programming?  If Fox News’ programming raises issues where a substantial portion of the American populace believes something their government is telling them is fact, and Fox uncovers information that casts doubt on the efficacy of these facts, it seems fair and balanced to me to report on that information, even if other news agencies decline to do so.  My opinion as to the identity of the misinformed citizen is the citizen who does not listen to arguments on both sides in order to make his decision about the truth.  The truth is exceedingly difficult to discover; nearly everyone has an agenda coloring it.  If you listen Only to Fox, you’re probably misinformed.  If you listen Only to CNN, Only to MSNBC, Only to CBS, you are probably misinformed.  Fair and Balanced is just a logo, just like Hope and Change is a logo.  My debate colleague asserted with claims of proof from several studies that Fox News is more ”in the misinformation industry” than others.  I successfully refuted - that is, cast substantial doubt on - three of the seven surveys, in fact the first three I investigated.  At that point I stopped investigating further.

 

POINTS

 

The reading I did, and wrote about below, brought to the forefront several important points.  Since I used them more than once, I introduce those points here:

 

Point 1. The internet has become a “chaotic information source”.  This means that for those who do not understand the rigorous definition of proof and how disciplined one must be to obtain real proof, one can ”prove” almost any assertion using internet resources.  For example, on the internet, it is straightforward to find an enormous amount of information that persuasively draws the conclusion that humans are a prominent cause of climate change on this Earth.  It is also straightforward to find lots of information that makes very compelling the notion that human-caused contributions are, in the aggregate, a completely trivial contributor to overall climate change.  So which is it?  What makes one large collection of information more factual than the other large collection?  When debaters find most or all of their ”evidence” on the internet, reasoned and logical dialog may well be extremely difficult.  They could well find, upon further research, that much of their evidence is not real evidence - that is not true, or at least possibly false.  Since it’s very difficult and expensive these days to avoid using the internet for most of one’s research, the inescapable conclusion is that the information sources must be analyzed also, not just the source’s content.  You have to be suspicious of everything.

 

Point 2. Correlation is Not cause.  To expand, this means that if you discover a correlation, even a strong one, between two events, you cannot a priori assume that one of the events causes the other event.  All too often, one later finds some other, different cause.

 

Point 3. One cannot draw conclusions and call them facts if the premises used to derive the conclusions are false or in question.  Of course, it’s common in politics these days to cite a premise as though it is true, even when the premise is *known* to be false.  As soon as the reader fails to question the premise, she can persuasively be led down a path to a false conclusion.  It can be really difficult to find false premises; the careful investigator must be on the lookout for them all the time.

 

Point 4. No one can declare a complex prediction true or false.  Relying on complex predictions is very precarious business.  Simple prediction: the Sun will rise tomorrow.  Indeed, there is a huge body of scientific evidence and data one can point to to conclude that this will occur.  Complex prediction: the value of gold will continue to rise for the next 3 years at least.  A lot of investors are saying this today, but they have been wrong before, and they will be wrong again.  If you don’t understand the nature of the complexities that cause huge uncertainty in complex predictions, read The Black Swan, it’s a great book.  The bottom line is, our economy, for example, is so complex that it is beyond the state of the art in science to understand it sufficiently to reliably make complex predictions about the future.  Of course, scientists serving politicians often do not want you to know this!  That does not make it less true.  (By the way, this is an area where I am, in fact, an acknowledged expert in my industry.  I know what I’m talking about.  Complex predictions are often little more than faith-based opinions.)

DISCUSSION

 

Ok.  Let’s start with the premise of the main article so that we have the proper background.  It says that Fox News viewers are “The Most Misinformed”.  I’ll take that to mean that Fox News viewers are more misinformed than viewers who predominantly watch other news sources like CNN, ABC, CBS, NBC, MSNBC, and perhaps a few others, I hope that’s ok.  Now let’s turn to the word misinformed.  I believe everyone in our debate agreed on the meaning of this word, which is that those who are misinformed believe statements to be true which turn out to be false (or vice versa).  A requirement is that the statements in question must clearly not be opinions.  Two people can have a different opinion and neither is misinformed.  Two people can be armed with the same set of facts, have arrived at different conclusions - opinions - from those facts, and still not be misinformed, unless one of the conclusions actually, unequivocally, logically derives from the facts at hand.  In a great many cases in politics, neither conclusion drawn by two people with different views has this property.  A simple example of someone who is misinformed is the person who believes that it is a fact that 2x2 is 5.  Since it has been proven that 2x2 is 4 (btw, this is a remarkably difficult proof requiring use of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra), we can say confidently that the person is misinformed.

 

And so, if you read the dialog, you discover that the debate is hinging on whether the information in the surveys permit one to derive as fact that Fox News viewers are more misinformed.

 

It’s trivially easy for me to stop the debate right here with the following, but rest assured I will not take this route.  This route is a cop out, but it is true nonetheless.  The route I could use is to leverage Point 2 that Correlation is Not cause.  The conclusion drawn is that Fox viewers are more misinformed.  Period.  However, the articles point to a small subset of the available information out there in the universe, that is, information on these five topics: global warming, health care, a mosque in New York City (ground zero), the Iraq war, and the 2010 election.  The surveys collectively conclude that on those five topics, Fox viewers are more misinformed.  Let us suppose for a moment that this is true.  Unfortunately for Mr. Mooney, it does not follow from this that Fox viewers are more misinformed.  Correlation can easily persuade someone that since, on the 5 important topics referenced, Fox viewers are more misinformed than other viewers, it follows that on some significant majority of topics, Fox viewers are more misinformed.  This is simply not the case, because that bit of correlation offers no insight whatsoever on the degree of misinformation with which Fox viewers vs. other viewers are endowed on the very next topic you might choose.  In fact, in the extreme, it is possible - no, I do not think it would be likely because these are significant issues of the day - but it is nonetheless possible that Mr. Mooney managed to pick the only five topics in the universe in which Fox viewers are more misinformed.  He draws the general conclusion anyway.  There are many, many more significant issues of the day.  Even if on these topics Fox viewers are more misinformed, there simply are no grounds to conclude that Fox viewers are more misinformed in general.

Statement 6: ”Allows a government panel to make decisions about the end-of-life care for people on Medicare.”  The survey declares that this statement is false.  Essentially, this is a reference to the Independent Payment Advisory Board (IPAB).  We need some information from that chaotic information source, the internet.  Let’s refer to the links as 2.6.1-2.6.3:

In 2.6.1, we learn what the IPAB is.  I defy anyone to read ObamaCare with sufficient care to determine for sure that wikipedia’s description is correct, but let’s assume that it is.  In particular, I invite you to read the section on Predicted Outcomes, where a list of benefits is cited.  These are the outcomes the Obama Administration wants you to believe.  However, we must be cautious: these benefits form a complex prediction.  I refer you to Point 4 above.  2.6.2 and 2.6.3 indicate that one can use the same law to predict a different, less sanguine set of future outcomes, in particular that IPAB will be required to ration health care resources to reign in the otherwise exploding cost of Medicare, which will otherwise swamp the federal budget.  In fact, in 2.6.3 we learn that this alternate prediction comes from, among others, a former advisor to Obama, Steven Rattner, while writing in the New York Times last September.  (In fact, he even called it Death Panels!)  Seems to me this is an expert witness, a person who even is biased to downplay death panels even if he knows that they’re in the law.

 

So which is it?  Death panels or no?  The answer is . . NEITHER.  Both are valid, possible, but complex, predictions from the available facts.  Neither is provably true or false, because they are complex predictions of a future that has not yet occurred.  There is sufficient complexity that no one can know with any confidence at all which prediction might eventually come true, or if another outcome not predicted yet will occur instead.

 

So our survey starts with a false premise: that there are no death panels in ObamaCare.  In reality, a reasonable and possible outcome of the law is IPAB-directed health care rationing.  We can draw no conclusion about whether Fox viewers are misinformed about the death panels statement, because since it is a complex prediction, we cannot yet know whether the statement will be true or false.

LINK 7

 

Ok, what survey should I look at next?  I can’t resist, I’m going to look at Link 7, the one referred to as the new one vindicating Jon Stewart.  After all, the blog says that this one is a tour-de-force! .. .. .. and indeed, it is methodical, contains a mountain of references, lots of math and statistics, and it’s enormous, it goes on and on, piling data on data on data.  A lot of work was done, and the paper shows it.

 

In summary, the paper takes as a fact anthropogenic global warming (AGW) of our planet.  It then draws extensive correlations between those who believe in AGW and their media viewing habits.  The paper’s authors find very troubling the number of people in the United States who do not believe in AGW.  It notes the high numbers of such people who watch, for example, Fox News.  The case they make is most compelling, though it cannot be entirely conclusive due to Point 2.  However, that lack of certainty in their conclusion is not my concern.

 

The paper crucially hinges on a single assertion: that there really is significant AGW.  More specifically, they assert that ”within the scientific community, broad international agreement has been reached about the reality and human causes of global climate change”.  Of all the 50 or more references in the paper, there is only one on this topic, ”IPCC”.  This is a report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change written in 2007 whose title is Summary for Policymakers.

 

With regard to this assertion, there are three possibilities.

  > The assertion is true (in which case Fox News viewers are misinformed)

  > The assertion is false (in which case those misinformed are CNN and MSNBC viewers)

  > Despite the claims that broad international agreement has been reached, we don’t actually know the truth of the assertion; the jury, as it were, is still out; if this is the case, then we still do not know who is misinformed, regardless of the paper’s claims, and we can argue that no viewer is misinformed on the topic.

 

So humor me for a minute if you happen to be someone who believes in AGW.  Let’s take a look at the only information source to which I have easy access again, that chaotic information source, the internet.

 

7.1 - http://www.prweb.com/releases/2012/12/prweb10222413.htm

7.2 - http://www.climatepolicy.org/?p=20

7.3 - http://www.nasa.gov/topics/earth/features/vapor_warming.html

7.4 - http://tucsoncitizen.com/wryheat/2012/03/19/water-vapor-and-the-climate-why-carbon-dioxide-is-a-very-minor-player/

7.5 - http://junksciencearchive.com/Greenhouse/Earth-s_Climate_Engine.pdf

7.6 - http://www.forbes.com/sites/peterferrara/2012/03/01/fakegate-the-obnoxious-fabrication-of-global-warming/

 

7.1 refutes IPCC claims about global warming’s effect on Earth’s ecosystems.  It is performed by scientists, Ph.D.’s in their fields, and their fields of expertise are applicable to the study. 

 

7.2 is an interesting, open discussion among scientists about whether there is broad consensus in these climate change results.  One amusing aspect is a concept called cherry picking, that is, selecting a subset of the available information and ignoring the rest, in order to ”prove” a point.  To be sure, cherry picking is unethical and bad science.  The trick is figuring out who is cherry picking.  Sometimes everybody is.

 

7.3 is a NASA study, done by NASA scientists, in which a collection of observations shows a positive feedback loop regarding water vapor and atmospheric warming, namely that warming produces more humidity, and since water vapor is known to be Earth’s most abundant greenhouse gas, this adds to the atmospheric warming.  That is, it feeds on itself.

 

7.4 is a description of studies in which experiments show a negative feedback loop regarding water vapor and atmospheric warming.  The experimental data is clearly at odds with the experimental data in 7.3.  Scientific studies can be very difficult to follow, but this one isn’t.  Read it, the data is crystal clear.  In fact, it corresponds with our experience: cities in dry climates are hotter, other things being equal.  Can 7.3 and 7.4 both be true?  That’s easy: both were experiments, so unless people are falsifying data (see 7.6, but nonetheless I doubt that), then yes, both are true.  Therefore, before we draw ultimate conclusions from either set of data, scientists must reconcile these data and understand the processes at work that appear to create a contradiction.  This is an inescapable logical conclusion from the observations.  Unfortunately, this has not yet been done.  These studies are recent, there just hasn’t yet been time.  Therefore, there is incomplete understanding in the scientific community of what is going on scientifically, regardless of anything the IPCC might say.  Another 7.4 makes is that if the data in 7.3 is true in general, our planet must be much hotter today than it actually is, due to the positive feedback loop.  Yet that’s not the case.  Again, we need to reconcile the data before we draw ultimate conclusions.

 

7.5 is referenced by 7.4.  I mention it partly because it reminds us that as recently as the 1970s, scientists were telling us so very strongly that the concern was not global warming; rather, it was global cooling.  This again points to contradictions in the data that are simply not adequately resolved yet by science.

 

I could go on, but again, this has taken hours.  There is so much more to understand, but no layman can possibly perform the effort with enough thoroughness.  What any layman can see is that there may not be a consensus at all, as Link 7 claims.  Furthermore, there are some persuasive scientific data that refute IPCC’s claims.  Data is the most powerful evidence that there is.  It trumps mathematical models, and it certainly trumps expert opinions.  All that models and opinions are good for is telling scientists where to gather data.  That data in these studies conflicts with IPCC’s results must be accounted for, and it has not been.  The endeavor to understand what’s happening simply is not scientific until that’s done.

 

7.6 is troubling.  In mid 2012 it was all over the news that substantial amounts of data associated with climate change, and that purportedly was strong evidence of AGW, was faked data.  Without going into details, data used by the IPCC in 2007 so that they could arrive at their conclusions is now called into serious question.  Link 7 was completed in 2011, and so predates this troubling information about the faked data.  7.6 also outlines strong motivation for faking data: there is much money to be made if AGW is significant, and very little money to be made if AGW is not significant.  You know what the movie says, Follow The Money.  A quote from 7.6: ”The real cutting edge in climate science was publicly exposed recently in a book by one of the long time leaders of the German environmental movement, Fritz Vahrenholt, in his new book, The Cold Sun. The book expresses the growing concern among more careful real climate scientists, rather than political scientists, that trends in solar activity portend a return to the cold, limited agricultural output, and widespread disease of the Little Ice Age, or even a more full blown, overdue by historical standards, real ice age.”  Wait.  You mean some of these scientists expressing conclusions about AGW in IPCC are not real climate scientists?  Of what value are conclusions from scientists who are not in the field?  Surely that’s a legitimate question.

 

In summary, it is clear that there is real doubt: IPCC had political motivations; only a small subset of IPCC scientists are climate specialists; we have a clear record of faked data; and we have climate specialists who offer data in contradiction to the IPCC’s findings, which has not yet been explained.  The last point is the strongest; it’s all about the data.  It quickly becomes clear that no one but an honest climate scientist who has the wherewithal to maintain the necessary knowledge in his field can understand which data is real, which is faked, how much international consensus there really is, whether it is political or not, whether it is financially motivated or not.  As a group funded by nations’ governments, the IPCC collectively have the financial motivation and the political means to lie about climate change consensus.  Most importantly, they have some climate scientists contributing to the IPCC body of work, but a great many of the scientists contributing to those results are not climate specialists.

 

I believe it is entirely reasonable to conclude from this information that no layman can yet know whether AGW is actually significant.  That being the case, asserting that Fox News viewers are misinformed on the topic is specious.  You see, what’s crucial here is that the authors of Link 7 also do not know whether AGW is true.  They made no effort to understand the controversy in any detail or explain these details in their paper; many of these details do predate their paper’s completion.  Instead, they assumed that AGW is truth.  Yet if that turns out to be a false premise of their paper, it undermines the entire paper!!  That this does not concern them and they wrote and published the paper anyway is completely damning of their scientific objectivity.

 

The bottom line is, while science might know the answer, politically the collective inhabitants of Earth do not yet know whether AGW is significant, even if many of them have decided (erroneously) that they do.  Writing a paper assuming that it is true is ridiculous; since the whole paper hinges on it, the paper means nothing unless the authors at least address the truth of the AGW assertion.

Ah, but in fact, this link is easy.  They gave away their bias right up front in a section called A Note on the Question of What is ’True’.  Apparently they were listening to my issues with Link 7.  Let us read exactly what they write on this subject.

 

”In the course of this study, to identify ’misinformation’ among voters, we used as reference points the conclusions of key government agencies that are run by professional experts and have a strong reputation for being immune to partisan influences.  These include the Congressional Budget Office, the Department of Commerce, and the National Academy of Sciences.  We also noted efforts to survey elite opinion, such as the regular survey of economists conducted by the Wall Street Journal; however, we only used this as supporting evidence for what constitutes expert opinion.”

 

Let me break that down for you.  The study authors decided to endow the CBO, DOC and NAS, all institutions funded by the government, with a ”strong reputation for being immune to partisan influences”.  I’ve become a student of these things in the past five years, and I can assure you that no such reputation is deserved.  Maybe democrats feel that way about these institutions, but most republicans don’t, and there is good reason, they misstate and exaggerate all the time.  The authors’ entire study, then, rests on their opinion that these institutions are above reproach.  This results in a circular argument!  The issues addressed in the remainder of the study are less likely to be valid unless these institutions are above reproach!  The second sentence above, that mentions the WSJ, is just funny.  It says that if independent institutions provide evidence supporting our opinion, then we include it!  JERKS.  May I assume, then, that if they provide evidence that does not support the study authors’ opinions, then they did not include that evidence?  I think it likely so.

 

How does this fundamental logic error manifest itself in the rest of the paper?  Let’s see.  ”Effect of Stimulus Legislation”.  CBO and WSJ produced studies saying that the stimulus legislation increased the number of full time-equivalent jobs by 2.0 to 5.2 million compared to what those amounts would have been otherwise.”  The study cites this as evidence that the Fox News viewer who believes the Stimulus did little for jobs is misinformed.  But that’s just not the case.  First, this is a complex prediction.  As we’ve previously discussed, even experts cannot reliably predict a complex future.  Secondly, the 2.0-5.2m number is not compared to the huge Stimulus cost of achieving those job numbers.  Third, the 2.0-5.2m number is not compared to the job growth we actually need in order to have a recovering economy (it’s a lot more).  Fourth, we have data - data! - from previous recessions that clearly show that a null response (government does nothing or very little) can and does allow an economy to recover much sooner than ours is recovering (which is, as yet, not at all, despite the Stimulus.)  See here:

5.1 http://www.smithsonianmag.com/history-archaeology/1907_Panic.html

5.2 http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1907

 

5.1 talks about the severity of the recession in 1907, which was significantly worse than 2008.  It talks about changes in complexity (2008 has higher complexity, faster speed, and greater scale) but fails to conclude - because no one can, it’s too complex to understand - what impact these complexity increases have on the situation.  (There’s an underlying lesson, though.  If people are informed about history and they have political motivation, like power, to ignore the lessons to be learned from that history, then they probably will find plenty of excuses to do so!)

 

5.2 indicates that the panic and recession lasted about a year.  So the recession was worse, but it recovered in a year.  Teddy Roosevelt basically did nothing and allowed the market to recover on its own.  Our current recession hasn’t recovered yet by most any measure.

 

So I rest my case.  Link 5 is another example of a false, or at least not provably true, premise.  If their premise turns out to be false, and we will not know this until well after the economy recovers, then the Fox News viewers were not misinformed after all.  It is folly to declare them misinformed now, because we’re talking about opinions, even expert opinions.  Not, alas, facts.
